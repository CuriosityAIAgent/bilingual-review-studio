# If the shock doesn't stick: Mapping out de-escalation

## The all-clear signal

Markets seem positioned for a prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz. That is clear in energy prices, rates and in the concentration of equity leadership. But if the path shifts toward de-escalation, the adjustment could be swift and underappreciated. A lower oil price feeds directly into falling yields and easier financial conditions, and could trigger a short-term rotation into parts of the market that investors have largely ignored.

It has become a familiar pattern: an Iran-related headline crosses the wire, oil prices spike, inflation fears resurface, and interest rates drift higher. Investors then crowd into the same trades: energy, defensive assets and mega-cap growth stocks. Part of the bid is grounded in fundamentals, but some of it is increasingly momentum-driven.

A plausible near-term path could involve a temporary extension of ceasefire conditions alongside a gradual resumption of transit. Over 100 million barrels remain effectively trapped in the region and could re-enter global markets in short order. One-fifth of global oil supply moves through the Strait of Hormuz.

## The feedback loop

The transmission mechanism is straightforward: energy prices feed into inflation, inflation expectations feed into policy odds, and policy expectations affect bond yields. An unwind in the oil price can push bond yields lower globally as investors reevaluate the need for interest rate hikes.

## Bracing for rotation

While the rally in the S&P 500 continues, the gains remain concentrated in artificial intelligence (AI) trades. Semiconductor, memory and computer stocks continue to drive returns, with some parts of the market trading near double where they were at the start of the year. By late May, AI data center beneficiaries had risen 40%, while the S&P 500 excluding AI gained just 4%.

S&P 500 earnings estimates in the first quarter stood at ~13% on a quarterly basis at the start of reporting season. Corporate earnings did not just beat those estimates; they jumped to over 28%. Excluding one-time gains, the figure is nearly 20%.

Cyclical sectors like financials, consumer discretionary, consumer staples and REITs remain relatively muted. A reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and an ensuing decline in both the oil price and bond yields could shift flows toward these segments, at least in the short term. If the shock fades, the unwind could be as swift as the initial pricing of risk.

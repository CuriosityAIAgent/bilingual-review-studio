# The market's inflation fears are running ahead of reality

## America's tightrope walk

As we look ahead to the remainder of 2026, it is clear that artificial intelligence, global fragmentation and inflation will continue to permeate economies and markets. Inflation, in particular, is all the more likely to re-settle at a stickier zone, potentially for years to come. Energy prices will likely command a more permanent geopolitical risk premium.

Investors started 2026 expecting the Federal Reserve to cut rates by half a percentage point. Those bets have evaporated, and 30-year U.S. bond yields have breached 5% again. But a shift in messaging from the Fed toward a renewed hiking cycle is unlikely to materialize. History shows, with the exceptions of the 1970s and 2022, the Fed is more likely to ease rates into oil shocks for fear of demand destruction.

In the face of robust economic data, and higher headline inflation currently running at 3.3% year-over-year, the Fed is more likely to be on hold. Despite not cutting rates, this could still end up being stimulative to risk assets as inflation-adjusted rates fall.

## Canary in the coal mine

The early signal might lie in Latin America. Due to the region's exposure to commodity fluctuations, monetary policy has been more reactive to inflationary risk. Over the past few decades, core Latin American central banks have shifted from policy takers to cycle leaders. The Bank of Brazil started raising rates in March of 2021, and the Bank of Mexico followed in June of the same year, ahead of the Fed and the European Central Bank.

Right now, inflation is within the bands of tolerance across most major Latin American economies. Using Latin American monetary policy as a guiding light for potential changes in global rates, the message is clear: we are not there yet.

## Pressure point

Economies facing a terms-of-trade shock on the back of higher energy prices, like the United Kingdom and the Eurozone, have seen markets pricing in as many as three rate hikes this year. The 70 basis point move in 10-year gilt yields is largely linked to the inflationary impact of the war. For the fifth year running, inflation sits well above the Bank of England's 2% target.

## The exception: a note on China

China stands apart: it does not face inflation risk, but instead exports deflationary pressure. Consumer inflation in the country remains subdued while producer prices continue to reflect excess capacity and weak pricing power.

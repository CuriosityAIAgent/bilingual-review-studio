# The U.S.–China relationship: A new equilibrium

The U.S.–China summit is not about a reset. It is about whether the world's two largest economies can make a fragile relationship more manageable. As trade flows have declined and supply chains have shifted, both sides have become more willing to test each other's vulnerabilities. For markets, less policy uncertainty would be a constructive signal, especially for global trade, China and broader emerging markets.

## The relationship has been damaged, but not replaced

The U.S.–China trade link has weakened, but it has not disappeared. U.S. goods exports to China fell 25.8% in 2025, while U.S. goods imports from China fell 29.7%. Even so, bilateral goods trade still totaled $414.7 billion last year, including $308.4 billion of U.S. imports from China and $106.3 billion of U.S. exports to China.

## The chokepoints now run both ways

Average U.S. effective tariffs on Chinese exports have risen to ~24%, while China's average effective tariffs on U.S. exports stand at ~32%. But tariffs are only one part of the leverage map. China accounts for roughly 70% of rare-earth mining, 90% of separation and processing, and 93% of magnet manufacturing. That concentration turns critical minerals into a macro issue, not just a supply-chain footnote.

## The Taiwan issue remains central

Taiwan remains the structural flashpoint in U.S.–China relations, sitting at the intersection of security, technology and semiconductor supply chains. One estimate suggests a Taiwan blockade could reduce U.S. GDP by 5% and China's GDP by 9%. That underscores why neither side benefits from an unmanaged crisis. China buys more than 80% of Iran's shipped oil, making Iran an energy-security issue for China and an inflation-risk issue for global markets.

## The relationship is global, not just bilateral

China's Belt and Road Initiative spans roughly 150 countries. Chinese engagement across Belt and Road countries reached record levels in 2025, including $128.4 billion in construction contracts and $85.2 billion in investment. For many countries, the challenge is not choosing one side on every issue; it is managing the costs of operating in a more fragmented global system.

For markets, the constructive signal is lower uncertainty. Fewer chances of abrupt policy shocks could support sentiment toward global trade, China and broader emerging markets. Dialogue does not mean a reset, but rather a test of whether the world's two largest economies can make a fragile relationship more predictable.

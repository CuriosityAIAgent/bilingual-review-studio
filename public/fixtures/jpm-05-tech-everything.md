# How tech became everything to everyone

When investors are excited about AI, they have bought tech. When they are worried about inflation, they bought tech. When looking for outperformance, they bought tech. The sector has been perceived as the answer to everything and everyone: a must-have portfolio allocation, both a cyclical and defensive trade, and the driver of earnings growth.

## Extraordinary earnings

Ahead of the first-quarter earnings season of 2026, the tech sector was expected to contribute nearly half of expected earnings growth, more than triple the estimate for the S&P 500 as a whole. Operating profit has surged, outpacing headcount additions, which in turn has fed margin expansion. Scale is working in their favor.

Cash holdings in the Magnificent 7 stocks, which now make up ~35% of the S&P 500, grew over 300% between 2011 and 2025. The combined cash and short-term investments held by the seven largest tech companies grew from roughly $140 billion in 2011 to $570 billion by early 2026.

## Tech's industrial era

At the helm of the AI wave, tech companies are driving a surge in capital expenditure on data centers, chips and energy infrastructure at a historic scale. This is beginning to resemble older industrial cycles, where growth depends less on asset-light scalability and more on the ability to deploy vast amounts of capital efficiently. For decades, tech's appeal was high capital returns and minimal reinvestment. Now it is the opposite.

Expectations for 2026 capital spending growth among the hyperscalers climbed from around 8% in April 2025 to 64% a year later. The S&P 500 overall rose from roughly 2% to 28%, pulled higher by those same hyperscalers, while the rest of the index crept from about 1% to just 8%.

## Technology, meet fragmentation

The infrastructure build-out has become increasingly synonymous with the U.S. economy's growth prospects. Paying a premium to fund a physical build-out on sovereign soil is more palatable than ever. The clearest example is the increased import of advanced technology products from Mexico at the expense of China's share. In 2024, China stood at $112 billion and Mexico at $102 billion. By 2025 the picture flipped: imports from China dropped to $62 billion, while imports from Mexico surged to $149 billion.

In a variety of macroeconomic regimes, technology has become the market's default answer, not because it fits neatly into any one category, but because it fits into all of them.

T Translation Studio

A governed AI translation workflow that learns from our team

It turns our English research into neutral Latin-American Spanish. The machine writes a first draft. Our people review and correct it. Those corrections stick, so the next document needs less fixing than the last. It's live now.

What it really is

Most "AI translation" hands you a draft and stops there. This keeps a person in charge and remembers what they fix. Every correction (a preferred term, a regionalism we want to avoid) becomes a governed rule and gets reused on the next document. The thing we're actually building isn't the translation. It's the memory and the audit trail behind it.

The workflow

You paste in English (a research note, a memo, an email), hit Translate, and watch it move through the pipeline. The first four steps are automated and take seconds. The last three are our people, each holding the document in turn and handing it down the chain.

How the memory is invoked

The team's approved terms, phrasings and "avoid this regionalism" rules aren't a clean-up at the end. They're loaded first and drive the whole process:

Loaded up front. Glossary, rules and prior translations load before anything is translated. Primes the first draft. Fed into the translator's instructions, so the draft already uses our wording.
Enforced throughout. Re-applied as hard constraints on every rewrite; the checks flag any drift. Feeds back in. A reviewer's correction becomes a governed rule, applied to every future document.

There's also a separate Train page: paste in a document we've already translated (English left, finished Spanish right) and it captures those pairings straight into the memory. So we can seed it from work the team has already done, not only from corrections made inside the tool.

The cross-model cycle, step by step

A model can't reliably catch its own mistakes. So a different AI reviews the first one's work, twice, and the document only gets rewritten where it genuinely needs it.

01  Draft — Sonnet writes. Claude Sonnet 4.6 drafts the Spanish, primed with our glossary and rules.
02  Score + critique — QE scores, GPT-5 critiques. Our Quality-Estimation model scores each segment 0–1 to route effort (it never approves). GPT-5, a different AI, returns the exact problems.
03  Refine — Sonnet rewrites using GPT-5's notes. It rewrites the flagged spans (told to leave good text alone); our rules re-applied as hard constraints.
04  Re-score + re-critique — QE re-scores, GPT-5 critiques again. Confirms the fix landed and nothing new broke.
05  Keep the best. Kept only if it removes a serious problem, or scores higher without adding one; otherwise reverted. Repeats until clean, with a hard cap. Good text is left alone.

In short: Sonnet writes › QE scores + GPT-5 critiques › Sonnet refines › QE re-scores + GPT-5 critiques again › keep the best. Because a worse rewrite gets discarded, the text only ever stays the same or gets better.

What's deterministic vs. what's AI

AI · JUDGMENT
The first-draft translation
The independent cross-model review
The QE routing score (where to focus effort)
DETERMINISTIC · RULES, NO AI
Number/currency checks ("billion" never becomes "billón")
Glossary & regionalism enforcement
The rule that QE/AI can never approve
An edit and hand-off log (changes appended, never overwritten)

The final Spanish is decided by the deterministic checks and a human sign-off, never by an AI's confidence score.
